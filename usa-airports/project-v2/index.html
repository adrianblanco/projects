To successfully fly from Point A to Point B without overpaying for a ticket, paying for a seat on the plane once you’ve already purchased your ticket, losing items to overzealous Transportation Security Administration officials, or getting stranded on the tarmac at departure or arrival requires great determination and more than a little luck.

Flyers hoping to rush home before the weekend may run into delay trouble. This chart illustrates which day of the week had the highest average departure delay time from 2015 to 2018.

For most of recent history, Thursdays have been the worst day to fly. Flyers over the last three years have had an average delay time of 11.24 minutes on Thursdays. A small delay like this may be annoying, but it’s nothing compared to 2018’s top Thursday delay – a whopping 2,482 minutes. In the span of that nightmare airport wait, a suffering commuter can watch around 60 episodes of a TV show or fly from New York to LA and back 4 times.

Another way of looking at the data is average delay by departure date. A calendar heatmap of departure delays by week of the year shows that domestic flights between the beginning of September and Thanksgiving have the lowest average departure delay times. Parents planning summer vacations around school break schedules should expect delays of 10 to 15 minutes for any domestic flight on pretty much any day of the week during those peak summer months. However, departing for home on a Sunday, rather than Saturday, may shave a few minutes off that departure delay time.
